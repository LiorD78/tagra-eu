Who must evaluate tachograph data from 2026 — a complete overview

By Truck Data Technology Updated April 2026 7 min read

Do you drive a truck or van? Do you run a company that transports goods across borders? From July 2026, the rules are changing — and they now apply to vans from 2.5 tonnes. Here is a clear overview of who is affected, whether you sit behind the wheel or in an office.

The key rule

Many operators assume that downloading data is sufficient. It is not. The law is clear: you must also regularly evaluate the data — checking driving times, breaks and rest periods — and archive it. Downloading without evaluation does not fulfil your legal obligation.

Operators already subject to the obligation today

If your vehicles already have digital tachographs, you are subject to the full evaluation and archiving requirement now. This includes:

Road hauliers (international and domestic) — vehicles over 3.5 t

Any operator using vehicles over 3.5 t for the carriage of goods must download, evaluate and archive tachograph data. This applies to both international and domestic transport.

Bus and coach operators

Operators of passenger vehicles with more than 8 seats used for regular or occasional services are subject to the same obligations as freight operators.

Self-employed (owner-operator) drivers

If you are a self-employed driver responsible for your own vehicle and data, you are the operator. The obligation applies to you personally.

New from 1 July 2026: vans in international transport

The obligation is now being extended to a new category of vehicles:

Van operators — 2.5–3.5 t in international transport

If your van (or van + trailer combination) has a GVW of 2.5–3.5 t and is used for the carriage of goods for hire or reward in international transport or cabotage, you must comply with tachograph rules from 1 July 2026.

Van drivers doing cabotage

Cabotage — carrying goods between two points within another EU country — triggers the obligation even if the journey is short. The van must have a tachograph and the operator must evaluate and archive data.

Who is not affected

Domestic transport only

If you operate exclusively within one country and do not cross borders with a commercial load, you are not affected by the July 2026 change.

Own-account transport (typically)

If you transport your own company's goods and driving is not your main profession, the obligation typically does not apply. However, the exact rules vary by country — verify with your national transport authority.

What "evaluate" actually means in practice

Evaluation is not a bureaucratic formality. It means systematically checking whether each driver has complied with legal limits on:

  • Daily driving time — maximum 9 hours (extendable to 10 hours twice per week)
  • Weekly driving time — maximum 56 hours
  • Breaks — 45 minutes after every 4.5 hours of driving (can be split 15+30)
  • Daily rest — minimum 11 hours (can be reduced to 9 hours three times between weekly rests)
  • Weekly rest — minimum 45 hours (can be reduced to 24 hours, with compensation)

In addition, many operators must also evaluate working time under the Working Time Directive — including time spent loading, waiting, and on call. TAGRA covers both EU 561/2006 and the Working Time Directive in a single evaluation.

Professional drivers: your own responsibility

If you drive professionally, you have a personal responsibility for your driver card data. Even if your employer handles the vehicle unit data, you should know what is on your card:

  • Your card must be downloaded at least every 28 days
  • You should be aware of any violations recorded before a roadside inspection
  • Keeping your own records gives you protection in case of disputes

What happens if you don't comply

Enforcement bodies across the EU actively inspect both at the roadside and at company premises. The consequences of non-compliance include:

  • Fines from the transport authority — up to €14,000 for tachograph violations
  • Fines from the labour inspectorate — up to €80,000 for working time violations
  • Risk of operating licence suspension for repeated violations
  • Liability in case of accidents where driving time violations are found
