Tachographs for vans: new obligation from July 2026

By Truck Data Technology Updated April 2026 8 min read

From 1 July 2026, the obligation to use a digital tachograph extends to light commercial vehicles with a gross vehicle weight of 2.5–3.5 t when used for international carriage of goods for hire or reward, or for cabotage within the EU. This is a major legislative change affecting a large number of operators and drivers who have not previously been subject to tachograph rules.

Deadline

1 July 2026. If your van is used for international transport for hire or reward, you need to have a digital tachograph installed and a compliance process in place before this date.

Who is affected?

The new obligation applies if all three of the following are true:

  1. Your vehicle has a gross vehicle weight (GVW) of 2.5–3.5 t (or your van plus trailer combination exceeds 2.5 t GVW)
  2. You carry goods for hire or reward (i.e. you are paid to transport goods, or you are a carrier providing transport services)
  3. You operate in international transport — crossing at least one EU border — or perform cabotage (carrying goods between two points within another EU member state)
What is cabotage?

Cabotage is the carriage of goods between two points within a single EU country by a carrier based in a different EU country. Example: a Czech carrier unloads in Hamburg and then picks up cargo for delivery to Berlin.

Who is NOT affected?

What you need to do

1. Install a digital tachograph

The tachograph must be installed by an authorised workshop. A calibration certificate is issued and must be kept with the vehicle. The tachograph must meet current EU technical standards — smart tachographs (G2V2) are required for newly registered vehicles from 2023.

2. Obtain the required cards

  • Driver card — each driver must have a personal driver card, issued by the national transport authority. Valid for 5 years.
  • Company card — the operator must have a company card, inserted in the tachograph when downloading vehicle unit data. Issued by the national transport authority. Valid for 5 years.

3. Set up a data management process

Once the tachograph is installed, you must comply with the same obligations as any other operator:

  • Download driver card data at least every 28 days
  • Download vehicle unit data at least every 90 days
  • Evaluate the downloaded data — check driving times, breaks and rest periods
  • Archive the data for at least 1 year

4. Ensure driver compliance

Drivers must observe the EU 561/2006 rules on driving times, breaks and rest periods. Enforcement bodies check compliance during roadside inspections and company checks.

What inspectors check at the roadside

During a roadside inspection, the enforcement officer will typically check:

  • That the tachograph is installed, functioning and calibrated
  • That the driver is using their driver card correctly
  • Driving times, breaks and rest periods for the current and previous days
  • That data has been downloaded within the legal deadlines

During a company inspection, inspectors will also check that data has been correctly evaluated and archived over a longer period.
